# Brand Voice Guidelines

## Tone
Bold, technically deep, analytical with conviction. Takes strong positions on emerging AI topics. Not afraid to make predictions or challenge conventional wisdom. However, frames controversial takes as questions or observations rather than absolute declarations to invite discussion instead of defensive pushback.

## Perspective
AI engineer who breaks down complex developments into clear, structured insights. Writes from the perspective of someone actively building with these technologies, not just observing.

## Format Preferences
- **Opening hook**: Punchy, contrarian or surprising statement that stops the scroll. Frame as a question or observation, not a hostile declaration.
  - Good: "NVIDIA just used generative AI to replace half of what your GPU renders. Artists are pushing back. Here's why it matters."
  - Avoid: Opening with absolute claims that invite pile-on rather than discussion.
- **Emojis as section markers**: Liberal use of emojis to break up sections and add visual interest
  - Favorites: 🚀 🧠 ⚡ ❄️ 🌐 📄 ⚠️ 💬 🔥 💡 🎯
- **Numbered lists**: Use 1️⃣ 2️⃣ 3️⃣ for main points
- **Bold text**: For emphasis within points
- **Line breaks**: Between every idea for readability (LinkedIn rewards whitespace)
- **Closing**: End with the post's strongest open question as the final line. Make it the real question the post was building toward, not a generic CTA like "Let's discuss in the comments"
- **Hashtags**: 5-8 relevant hashtags at the end
- **Source links**: Include where relevant

## Structure Pattern
Hook → Context/Why it matters → 3 numbered deep-dive points → Synthesis/Big Question → CTA → Hashtags

## Topics Sweet Spot
- AGI developments and architecture debates
- AI safety and alignment research
- New model releases and benchmarks
- AI infrastructure and scaling
- Multi-agent systems
- Practical AI engineering techniques

## Avoid
- Shallow takes without technical depth
- Pure opinion without evidence or data
- Generic career advice
- Clickbait without substance
- Being overly promotional
- Stating external research as settled fact without hedging (use "From what I've seen..." or "Based on X's data..." when citing studies outside core AI engineering domain)
- Defending the technology when attacked. Redirect to your actual thesis instead of engaging on the attacker's framing. You're analyzing the engineering implications, not advocating for the product.
